For Teachers

The 4-Player Format

Every game in this collection is designed for four active participants at a time. This reflects how improv-style drama games work best: small enough for clarity, large enough for ensemble dynamics.

Running Games with Your Class

  • Volunteer groups: Ask for four student volunteers to play a single round while the rest of the class watches. Rotate volunteers between rounds.
  • Teams of four: Divide the class into teams of four and run rounds simultaneously or back-to-back. All students stay active.
  • Odd numbers: If you have 3 students, one player can take two roles or you can step in as the fourth. If you have 5, a fifth player can be a silent observer who swaps in next round.

Difficulty Is in the Prompt, Not the Game

Any game in this collection can be played by any year level — what changes is the prompt you give them. Each game has three prompt tabs:

  • Beginner — designed for Years 7 & 8. Familiar, fun scenarios with low stakes. Great for students new to drama games.
  • Intermediate — suited to Years 9 & 10. More complex situations that reward character commitment and ensemble awareness.
  • Advanced — pitched at Years 11 & 12. Richer scenarios with subtext, nuance, and real dramatic stakes — but always with room for humour.

Start with a Beginner prompt even with older students when introducing a new game — it lets them learn the rules without the added pressure of a harder scenario.

Projector Mode

Open any game and tap Projector Mode to display the setup and instructions on a class screen. Students can see the rules while you facilitate. The built-in timer helps keep rounds on track.

Using the Filters

  • Energy: High-energy games need space and movement. Low-energy games suit quieter, focused moments.
  • Skills: Filter by the outcome you want to develop — voice, physicality, storytelling, and more.
